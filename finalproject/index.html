Leelanau County

A Quick History Lesson

The area that would come to be known as Leelanau Country was first recognized by the Michigan Legislature around the year 1840, but would not become its own legal entity until the year 1863. During this time area would be settled that would eventually become towns such as Leeland, Empire, Suttons Bay, and many others were initially settled. There are scattered areas within the county that are controlled by the Grand Traverse Band of Ottawa and Chippewa Indians, though these are primarily concentrated around the village of Suttons Bay. While the county began with what could be classfied as an agrarian economy, with aspects of a seaport due to the surrounding lake. Leelanau has transitioned, into a tourist/retirement based economy in the past 20 years, who flock to the county's plethora of beach communities.

The Major Towns

Whenever one talks about Leelanau County, a few places tend to come to people's minds.

Suttons Bay

Suttons Bay is a Civic Township that is located in between Traverse City and Northport. It is Leelanau's County Seat, and serves as a gateway to the county at large for many tourists.

Northport

What do Do?

There is a saying amonst locals, that if you're bored in Leelanau it's because you're either at work, or not looking hard enough. The region is known for it's breathtaking views, plethora of hiking, biking, and scenic trails. As well as world class golf courses, and some of the midwest's best skiing. And if the outdoors isn't your thing, the region also boasts an up and coming food, wine, and spirits scene that is quickly becoming recognized as among the best in the world.

The Question of the Future

The question of what the future has in store for Leelanau is, to put it mildly, a contested issue. With the rise in population that has occured in the 2020s, there is an increasing presure on the county to develop, much to the chagrin of those who've called the area home for decades. The push back largely centers around the use of land to develop new housing and infrastructure for those who are moving to the area. With many citing the development/suburbanization of once bucolic areas in the southern part of the state as their concern for the future of Leelanau. The Leelanau Conservancy is an organization that is working to counter this to mixed success. As of now it's difficult to say which side will win out, though the "limited" nature of the Baby Boomer generation's time, relative to the quantity of people who will replace them is often seen as the strongest argument against allowing for more development than has already occurred.
